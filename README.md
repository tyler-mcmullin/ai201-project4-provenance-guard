# AI201 Prject 4: Provenance Guard

An API for detecting whether a piece of text was written by a human or generated by an AI without making binary accusations.

---

## Content Submission Endpoint

`POST /submit` accepts a JSON body with a `text` field and returns a structured response including the attribution result, confidence score, transparency label text, and both individual signal scores.

**Example request:**
```bash
curl -X POST http://localhost:8080/submit \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{"text": "Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift in modern society. It is important to note that while the benefits of AI are numerous, it is equally essential to consider the ethical implications.", "creator_id": "test-user-1"}'
```

**Example response:**
```json
{
  "attribution": "ai",
  "confidence_score": 0.7312,
  "content_id": "8a03f9ac-cec3-4216-982d-2f2fa1186a2c",
  "creator_id": "test-user-1",
  "label_text": "Our tools indicate this content was likely AI-generated. Our signals suggest AI authorship with high confidence. If you created this content yourself, you can submit an appeal and a reviewer will examine your submission.",
  "signals": {
    "llm_score": 0.8,
    "stylometric_score": 0.5706
  },
  "status": "complete"
}
```

---

## Multi-Signal Detection Pipeline


### Signal 1 — LLM-Based Classification (Groq)

**What it measures:** Semantic and stylistic coherence as perceived by a language model. The classifier reads the text holistically and assesses whether the overall voice, idea progression, phrasing choices, and tonal consistency match patterns associated with human or AI authorship. It also looks for common AI signatures such as em dashes, filler phrases like "it is important to note," and uniform paragraph structure.

**Output:** A float between 0.0 (certainly human) and 1.0 (certainly AI), returned as `llm_score`.

**What it misses:** Highly polished human writing like academic papers or professional journalism can read as AI to the classifier. Also, AI text that has been lightly edited by a human may read as human. Short texts also give the model too little signal to be reliable.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric Heuristics (Python)

**What it measures:** Three measurable statistical properties of text surface structure:

- **Sentence length variance:** the standard deviation of word counts across sentences. AI text tends to cluster around a comfortable middle range; human writing is more variable.
- **Punctuation variety:** How many distinct punctuation types appear. AI text uses punctuation conventionally and sparingly while human writers use theirs more freely.
- **Average sentence complexity:** Average words per sentence as a proxy for syntactic depth. Very uniform complexity is AI-like.

**Output:** A float between 0.0 and 1.0, returned as `stylometric_score`. Each sub-metric is normalized individually and the three are averaged equally.

**What it misses:** Writers who naturally write in a regular, clear style will score as AI. Genre matters heavily since technical documentation is stylometrically similar to AI output. The signal is almost impossible to use on texts with fewer than two sentences.

Both signal scores are returned in every API response alongside the combined confidence score, so callers can see how each signal contributed to the result.

---

## Confidence Scoring

### How signals are combined

The two signals are combined using a weighted average — 70% LLM, 30% stylometric — reflecting the LLM signal's broader coverage of semantic and stylistic properties. The stylometric signal proved noisy on short texts during testing, which is why it carries less weight.

```
combined = (llm_score * 0.70) + (stylometric_score * 0.30)
```

A disagreement penalty fires when the two signals differ by more than 0.45. When they disagree significantly, the combined score is pulled toward 0.5 to reflect the uncertainty in the system rather than averaging through it:

```
if |llm_score - stylometric_score| > 0.45:
    pull = (disagreement - 0.30) × 0.5
    combined = combined ± pull  (toward 0.5)
```

### Thresholds

| Combined score | Attribution |
|---|---|
| ≥ 0.70 | `ai` |
| 0.31 – 0.69 | `uncertain` |
| ≤ 0.30 | `human` |

### What a score means

A score of 0.5 represents maximum uncertainty. A score of 0.75 means both signals lean AI and agree closely enough for a confident label. A score of 0.15 means both signals strongly indicate human authorship.

### Validation — two example submissions with different confidence levels

**High-confidence AI**

Input: *"Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift in modern society. It is important to note that while the benefits of AI are numerous, it is equally essential to consider the ethical implications. Furthermore, stakeholders across various sectors must collaborate to ensure responsible deployment."*

```json
{
  "attribution": "ai",
  "confidence_score": 0.7312,
  "signals": { "llm_score": 0.8, "stylometric_score": 0.5706 }
}
```

**Lower-confidence / Uncertain**

Input: *"I've been thinking a lot about remote work lately. There are genuine tradeoffs — flexibility and no commute on one side, isolation and blurred work-life boundaries on the other. Studies show productivity varies widely by individual and role type."*

```json
{
  "attribution": "uncertain",
  "confidence_score": 0.6403,
  "signals": {
    "llm_score": 0.7, "stylometric_score": 0.5011
  },
}
```

The first submission produces a confident AI label and the second correctly reflects the ambiguity introduced by the stylometric signal's difficulty with short informal texts.

To validate that scores are meaningful, several inputs were tested that should produce clearly different results: known AI-generated paragraphs (corporate buzzword-heavy prose, AI blog post openers), known human-written text (casual personal writing with errors and contractions), and borderline cases (formal academic writing). The AI paragraphs consistently scored above 0.70; the casual human writing consistently scored below 0.35. The formal academic writing scored in the uncertain range, which is the expected behavior given the known limitation described below.

## Transparency Labels

The exact text displayed to a reader for each of the three variants:

**High-confidence AI** (combined score ≥ 0.70):

> "Our tools indicate this content was likely AI-generated. Our signals suggest AI authorship with high confidence. If you created this content yourself, you can submit an appeal and a reviewer will examine your submission."

**High-confidence Human** (combined score ≤ 0.30):

> "Our tools indicate this content was likely written by a human. Our signals suggest human authorship with high confidence."

**Uncertain** (combined score between 0.31 and 0.69):

> "We're not sure. Our tools found some patterns that sometimes appear in AI-generated writing, but not enough to reach a confident conclusion. This content has been marked as uncertain. If you created this content yourself, you can submit an appeal and a reviewer will examine your submission."

The labels use plain language throughout with no references to classifiers, scores, or model outputs. A non-technical reader sees what the system concluded and what they can do about it. The uncertain label deliberately avoids saying the content is AI-generated, because it isn't confident enough to say so.

## Appeals Workflow

Creators can contest a classification by submitting a `POST /appeal` request with their `content_id` and a written explanation.

**Example request:**
```bash
curl -X POST http://localhost:8080/appeal \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{
    "content_id": "8a03f9ac-cec3-4216-982d-2f2fa1186a2c",
    "creator_id": "test-user-1",
    "creator_reasoning": "I wrote this myself from personal experience. I am a non-native English speaker and my writing style may appear more formal than typical."
  }'
```

**Example response:**
```json
{
  "appeal_id": "fa023f01-1cad-4042-a649-c51b91adba60",
  "content_id": "8a03f9ac-cec3-4216-982d-2f2fa1186a2c",
  "creator_id": "test-user-1",
  "message": "Your appeal was received and is under review.",
  "status": "under_review"
}
```

When an appeal is received the system logs the appeal entry alongside the original attribution decision (linked by `content_id`), records the creator's reasoning, and stores the original attribution result and confidence score in the appeal entry so a human reviewer has full context without needing to cross-reference. A human reviewer is then able to consult the audit log and make a judgment.

## Rate Limiting

**Limits:** 10 requests per minute, 100 requests per day, per IP address.

**Reasoning:** Each submission triggers a Groq API call, so costs scale directly with request volume. A writer checking their own work rarely needs more than a few attributions per minute in an interactive context. 10 per minute is permissive for normal use while being strict enough to deter bulk scraping or classifier probing (submitting many variations to reverse-engineer the detection threshold). The 100 per day cap prevents sustained automated abuse while leaving room for an active user to not be blocked completely.

**Rate limit in action** — sending 12 rapid requests produces 10 `200` responses followed by `429` responses:

```
for i in $(seq 1 12); do
  curl -s -o /dev/null -w "%{http_code}\n" -X POST http://localhost:5000/submit \
```

## Known Limitations

**Formal and technical human writing will frequently score as uncertain or AI-generated.** The stylometric signal measures uniformity through consistent sentence length, low punctuation variety, predictable complexity and determines it to be AI-like even though this is also exactly how competent technical writers write. A human writing API documentation, an academic paper, or a policy memo naturally produces text that scores high on every stylometric sub-metric the system uses. The LLM signal partially compensates by reading for semantics, but formal registers tend to resemble AI output. Technical writers and non-native English speakers who write in careful, regular prose are the users most likely to receive uncertain or false-positive AI labels and need to use the appeals workflow.

## Spec Reflection

**Where the spec helped:** The planning document's requirement to write out all three label variants before building was directly useful. Having the exact strings written down in `planning.md` before touching the code meant `generate_label()` was essentially a lookup function rather than a design decision made under implementation pressure. The labels did not change between planning and shipping.

**Where implementation diverged:** The spec defined the confidence scoring as a 60/40 weighted average (LLM/stylometric) with a disagreement penalty firing at a difference of 0.30. During testing, the stylometric signal was consistently scoring highly confident as AI even though it was not. It was found that the stylometric signals were unreliable for text this short. Type to token ratio was removed entirely from the stylometric signal, the LLM weight was raised to 70/30, and the disagreement threshold was raised to 0.45.

## AI Usage

**Instance 1 — Generating the stylometric signal function**

The stylometric signal function was generated by providing the detection signals section of `planning.md` and the architecture diagram as context, then asking for a standalone `stylometric_signal(text) -> float` function that implemented the four sub-metrics (sentence length variance, TTR, punctuation density, sentence complexity). The AI produced a working function with reasonable normalization ranges. What was revised: TTR was removed entirely after testing revealed it was unreliable on short texts.The normalization ranges for the remaining sub-metrics were also adjusted after running debug output on known AI and human texts and seeing which sub-metrics were causing issues.

**Instance 2 — Generating the confidence scoring function**

The confidence scoring function was generated by providing the uncertainty representation section of `planning.md` specifying the 60/40 weighting, the disagreement penalty, and the three threshold bands. The AI produced a function that matched the spec. What was revised: the weighting was changed from 60/40 to 70/30 and the disagreement threshold from 0.30 to 0.45 after observing that the stylometric signal's middle-range scores were pulling the combined score into the uncertain category on inputs that the LLM classified confidently.
